Can Your Habits Predict Your GPA?

What student lifestyle data reveals about academic performance and what it cannot tell us.

Haoran Chen, Ian Basora, Zen Sadler

View Technical Report and Code Back to Projects

The Question

Academic performance is one of the most studied outcomes in education research, but the factors that predict it are still complex. Attendance, study habits, and student engagement can be connected to grades, while income, prior academic history, and outside responsibilities can also shape a student's performance.

This project asks a practical version of that question: using a real undergraduate student dataset, can a machine learning model predict whether a student passes or fails based on lifestyle habits and academic background?

This question matters because early identification of academic risk could help advisors reach students before they fall too far behind. At the same time, this type of model has to be handled carefully because it uses sensitive student information. The goal should be support, not judgment.

Research questions

  • Can behavioral factors such as preparation time, attendance, and gaming habits help predict pass or fail outcomes?
  • Does having a part time job or lower family income appear as a disadvantage in the model?
  • Do prior academic scores outweigh lifestyle factors when predicting current GPA?

The Data

This project uses the Student Performance Metrics Dataset published in October 2024 by researchers at Universiti Malaya in Malaysia. The data was collected through a direct survey of 493 undergraduate students across multiple academic departments. Unlike many classroom datasets, it was not already cleaned for this project, so preprocessing decisions were necessary before modeling.

The dataset contains variables related to department, gender, SSC and HSC prior scores, family income, hometown, computer proficiency, preparation time, gaming habits, attendance percentage, part time job status, extracurricular activity, English proficiency, last semester performance, and current CGPA.

Because the original outcome was a continuous GPA value, we converted it into a binary pass or fail label using 2.5 as the cutoff. This created an imbalanced target, with about 87 percent passing and 13 percent failing. That imbalance matters because a model that predicts every student as passing would look accurate without actually identifying the students who need support.

What the Models Found

Four classification models were trained and compared: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, and XGBoost. Logistic Regression served as a simple baseline, Decision Tree gave a more interpretable tree structure, Random Forest improved performance by combining many trees, and XGBoost was used as a more advanced method beyond what was directly covered in class.

Random Forest and XGBoost performed better than the simpler models. The clearest interpretation came from the Random Forest results, so the discussion below focuses on that model while still using the full model comparison to support the conclusions.

Question 1: Can habits predict pass or fail?

Yes, but habits should be interpreted carefully. Attendance, preparation time, and gaming hours all showed meaningful relationships with pass or fail outcomes. Students with stronger attendance and more preparation time generally performed better, while students with very low attendance or higher gaming hours showed more risk.

Question 2: Does income or employment status show up as a disadvantage?

Partially. Family income and part time job status appeared in the feature set, but they were not the strongest direct predictors. The more important pattern is that students facing financial pressure may also have less time, lower attendance, longer commutes, or competing responsibilities outside school. In that sense, income can influence the model indirectly through the behavioral variables that the model weights more strongly.

Question 3: Do prior scores outweigh lifestyle factors?

Prior academic performance mattered, especially last semester GPA. However, lifestyle variables were not irrelevant. The correlation heatmap shows that recent academic performance is the strongest signal, while attendance, preparation, English proficiency, and other behavioral or academic support factors still add information.

What the Random Forest model revealed

The Random Forest feature importance chart shows which variables the model relied on most. Last semester GPA was the strongest predictor, which makes sense because recent academic performance often carries forward. The concern is that this also makes the model less useful as an early warning tool. If the model mostly identifies students who were already struggling before, then it may simply repeat what the school already knows.

The more useful signals are the ones that can lead to support before a grade problem becomes severe. Attendance, preparation, gaming habits, and academic support variables are more actionable than prior GPA, but they still need human context before any decision is made.

Model evaluation

The confusion matrix shows how the Random Forest model performed on the test set. The model correctly identified most failing students, which is important because missing an at risk student is the more serious error in this project. Because the dataset is imbalanced, F1 score and recall are more meaningful than accuracy alone.

Ethical Risks and Responsible Use

A harmful side of this project is that a prediction model could turn student support into student labeling. If a school used the model carelessly, students could be marked as at risk based on attendance, income, work status, or past grades without anyone asking what is actually happening in their lives.

Low attendance is a good example. A student might miss class because of work, illness, transportation problems, caregiving responsibilities, or financial pressure. If the model treats that student as unmotivated, the result could be unfair and harmful. The model sees a pattern, but it does not understand the reason behind that pattern.

Income and family background create another risk. A model may learn that lower income students are more likely to struggle, but that does not mean lower income students have less ability. It may only show that they face more outside pressure. If schools used this prediction to lower expectations, restrict opportunities, or judge students before offering support, the model would reinforce inequality instead of reducing it.

Responsible use: A prediction should lead to supportive outreach, tutoring options, advising, financial help, or attendance support. It should not be used to punish students, remove opportunities, or make assumptions about effort.

The safest way to use a model like this is as an early conversation starter, not as a final decision. Human review is necessary because the model can show patterns, but it cannot understand a student's full situation. The ethical value of this project depends less on whether the model can predict academic risk and more on how people respond to that prediction.

Limitations and What We Would Do Differently

The largest limitation is scope. The dataset represents 493 students at one university in Malaysia. Academic systems, grading expectations, cultural norms, and student support structures differ across institutions and countries, so the findings should not be generalized without validation on new data.

The dataset is also a single snapshot. Each student appears once, so the model cannot detect changes over time. A student whose attendance dropped suddenly after a personal issue looks the same as a student who had low attendance all semester. Longitudinal data would make the model more useful for early intervention.

The binary pass or fail label also removes important detail. A student with a 2.4 CGPA and a student with a much lower CGPA are both labeled as failing, but they may need different types of support. A future version could predict actual GPA or separate students into multiple levels of academic risk.

Finally, this project identifies correlation, not causation. Attendance and preparation are connected to passing, but the model cannot prove that changing one behavior alone would cause a student to pass. Other factors such as mental health, transportation, employment, family responsibilities, and course difficulty may influence both habits and grades.
