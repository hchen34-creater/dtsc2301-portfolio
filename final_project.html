DTSC 2302 Final Project

Can Your Habits Predict Your GPA?

What student lifestyle data reveals about academic performance and what it cannot tell us.

Haoran Chen, Ian Basora, Zen SadlerSpring 2026
01 The Question

Why This Project Matters

Academic performance is often discussed as if it only depends on effort, but the reality is more complicated. Attendance, preparation time, prior scores, family income, work status, and access to resources can all shape how students perform in school.

This project asks whether a machine learning model can predict whether a student passes or fails using lifestyle habits and academic background. The goal is not to judge students. The goal is to understand whether data can help identify academic risk early enough for support to happen sooner.

Research questions:
  • Can preparation time, attendance, and gaming habits help predict pass or fail outcomes?
  • Does lower family income or part time work appear as a disadvantage in the model?
  • Do prior academic scores matter more than lifestyle factors?
02 The Data

Student Performance Metrics Dataset

The dataset contains survey records from 493 undergraduate students at Universiti Malaya in Malaysia. It includes department, gender, SSC and HSC scores, income level, hometown, computer proficiency, preparation time, gaming habits, attendance, part time job status, extracurricular activity, English proficiency, last semester performance, and current CGPA.

Because the original outcome was CGPA, we converted it into a pass or fail label using 2.5 as the cutoff. This created a class imbalance, so model evaluation focused on finding failing students rather than accuracy alone.

03 What We Found

Three Models Compared

We trained and compared Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost. Logistic Regression gave us a baseline. Random Forest gave the strongest fail F1 score and a clear feature importance view. XGBoost caught every failing student in the test set, which matters if the goal is early support.

04 Ethics and Limits

What Could Go Wrong

A harmful side of this project is that a prediction could turn into a label. If a school used the model carelessly, students could be marked as at risk based on income, attendance, job status, or past grades without anyone asking what is actually happening in their lives.

Low attendance might come from work, illness, transportation problems, caregiving responsibilities, or financial pressure. If the model treats that student as unmotivated, the result could be unfair and harmful.

Family income also needs care because it can act as a proxy for broader conditions such as housing stability, food security, commute length, and access to resources. The model should never be used to lower expectations or limit opportunities.

Responsible use: a prediction should lead to a supportive conversation, tutoring, advising, attendance support, or financial resources. It should not be used as punishment or as a final judgment about a student.
