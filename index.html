About Me

My name is Haoran Chen and I am taking data science at UNC Charlotte. Before these classes, I thought data science was mostly about writing code and making charts. It turned out to be a lot more complicated and way more interesting than I expected.

Why This Matters

When people talk about data science, it is easy to imagine something purely technical: code, charts, predictions, dashboards. But the reason I am excited to learn it is that many of the questions I care about are social questions.

Education, health, employment, housing, and public policy are all shaped by human choices and systems. Data science gives us tools to look at patterns across a community instead of only seeing individual cases.

At the same time, modeling social topics and people comes with real responsibility. People are not just rows in a spreadsheet, and data is never a perfect mirror of reality.

Essay: Defining Data Science

Why learning data science in social and interdisciplinary contexts matters

Before these classes, if you asked me to define data science I probably would have said something like using computers to analyze data. That is not wrong exactly, but it misses the point.

Data science is finding patterns in information, being honest about uncertainty, and making sure the work actually serves people.

What I have come to understand is that data science is really three things at once. It is a technical skill set, a scientific process, and a social practice. Data comes from people, affects people, and always exists inside a context.

Using data about people makes me think about privacy and consent first, but it goes beyond that. Responsibility also means being transparent about limitations, avoiding overconfident claims, and checking whether results affect groups differently.
