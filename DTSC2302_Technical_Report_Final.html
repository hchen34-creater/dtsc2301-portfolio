Can Your Habits Predict Your GPA?

A technical walkthrough of dataset source, preprocessing choices, model experiments, evaluation, and ethical limits for a pass or fail classification task.

01 Dataset and Problem Framing

Dataset Overview

The project uses the Student Performance Metrics Dataset by Hasan, Hasan, and Manzoor, published through Mendeley Data in 2024. The dataset contains survey records from undergraduate students at Universiti Malaya in Malaysia. It fits the project requirement because it is not from Kaggle or the UCI Repository, and it required real cleaning and modeling choices before analysis.

The main question is whether student lifestyle patterns and academic background can help predict whether a student passes or fails. The target was created from Overall CGPA. Students with Overall CGPA of at least 2.5 were labeled as pass. Students below 2.5 were labeled as fail.

02 Preprocessing and Assumptions

Preparing the Data for Modeling

The preprocessing workflow began by loading the raw CSV file and checking the row count, column names, data types, and missing values. The notebook includes cleaning steps even though the uploaded version showed no missing values, because the workflow should still run safely if a future version of the dataset contains blanks.

StepWhat was doneWhy it matters
Missing valuesNumeric blanks were handled with median values and categorical blanks were handled with the most common category.This keeps the workflow reproducible and avoids silently dropping student records.
Target creationOverall CGPA was converted into pass_fail using 2.5 as the cutoff.This changed the project from predicting an exact GPA to identifying academic risk.
Leakage controlOverall CGPA was removed from the predictors after creating the target.Keeping it would give the model the answer and make the evaluation invalid.
Categorical variablesDepartment, gender, income, hometown, preparation, gaming, attendance, job status, extracurricular status, and semester were one hot encoded.Machine learning models need numeric inputs, and encoding lets the categories be used without forcing an artificial order.
ScalingStandard scaling was applied after encoding.This was especially important for Logistic Regression because it is sensitive to variable scale.
Train test splitAn 80 percent training and 20 percent testing split was used with stratification.Stratification keeps the pass and fail proportions similar in both sets.
Key assumption: the pass or fail threshold is a simplification. A student with a 2.49 GPA and a student with a much lower GPA are both labeled as fail, even though their academic situations are different.

05 Evaluation and Interpretation

Why Fail Recall and F1 Matter

Accuracy is not enough for this project because the pass group is much larger than the fail group. The more useful question is whether the model identifies the students who actually fail. For that reason, fail recall and fail F1 are more meaningful than accuracy alone.

From a student support perspective, XGBoost has an important advantage because it missed no failing students in the test set. Random Forest still had the strongest fail F1 score, which means it balanced finding failing students with avoiding extra alerts. Both are useful, but the right model depends on how the prediction would be used.

06 Feature Importance

What the Models Relied On

Random Forest and XGBoost both showed that last semester performance was one of the strongest predictors. That makes sense because recent academic performance is closely tied to current CGPA. The more useful finding is that attendance, preparation, gaming, English proficiency, and computer proficiency also appeared as meaningful signals.

Main interpretation: prior academic performance matters, but the lifestyle variables are not irrelevant. The model results connect back to the public post because habits can act as early signals, as long as they are interpreted carefully.
07 Limitations, Ethics, and Responsible Use

What Could Go Wrong

The biggest limitation is that this dataset represents 493 students from one university in Malaysia. The results should not be generalized to other colleges without testing the model on local data. Academic systems, attendance expectations, grading practices, and economic conditions vary across institutions.

The model also shows correlation, not causation. Attendance and preparation time are connected to pass or fail outcomes, but the model cannot prove that changing one behavior alone would cause a student to pass. Other factors such as mental health, work hours, transportation, family pressure, and course difficulty may influence both habits and grades.

The harmful side of this project is that a prediction could turn into a label. If a school used the model carelessly, students could be marked as at risk based on income, attendance, job status, or past grades without anyone asking what is actually happening in their lives. Low attendance might reflect illness, work, caregiving, or transportation problems rather than low motivation.

Family income is especially sensitive. It can act as a proxy for housing stability, food security, commute length, and access to academic resources. Using it as a direct predictor could reproduce inequality if the output is used to judge students instead of support them.

Responsible use: predictions should be used as a starting point for supportive outreach, not as a final judgment. A real system should include human review, privacy protection, fairness checks across groups, and clear limits on how the prediction can be used.
